Who was Jomo Kenyatta?

Born Kamau wa Ngengi in the rolling Gatundu hills of central Kenya, Jomo Kenyatta rose from a humble Kikuyu upbringing to become one of Africa's most consequential leaders of the twentieth century. He was educated in mission schools, studied anthropology at the London School of Economics, and absorbed the ideas of the pan-African movement in wartime Britain before returning home to lead his people to freedom.

Detained by the British colonial government for nine years on charges related to the Mau Mau uprising — charges he always denied — Kenyatta emerged from prison not embittered but resolute. He negotiated Kenya's independence, which came on 12 December 1963, and led the country as its first Prime Minister and then first President until his death in 1978. He was universally known as Mzee, a Swahili title of deep respect meaning “wise elder.”

A life of landmarks

  • 1920s — Joins the Kikuyu Central Association and begins campaigning against colonial land seizures, emerging as a leading African political voice.
  • 1929 — Travels to London to present Kikuyu grievances to the Colonial Office — the first of many journeys that would shape his worldview.
  • 1938 — Publishes Facing Mount Kenya, a landmark anthropological study of Kikuyu society. It remains one of the foundational texts of African literature.
  • 1945 — Co-organises the Fifth Pan-African Congress in Manchester alongside Kwame Nkrumah and W.E.B. Du Bois, making the intellectual case for African self-determination before the world.
  • 1947 — Returns to Kenya and becomes President of the Kenya African Union (KAU), massively expanding its membership and anti-colonial agitation.
  • 1952–61 — Arrested and convicted in a contested trial; serves nine years of detention in remote Lokitaung under colonial orders.
  • 1963 — Leads KANU to victory in pre-independence elections. On 12 December, Kenya achieves Uhuru — independence — and Kenyatta becomes the first Prime Minister.
  • 1964 — Kenya becomes a republic; Kenyatta is sworn in as first President, a position he holds for fourteen years.
  • 1964–78 — Champions the Harambee (“pulling together”) philosophy, mobilising communities to build thousands of schools, clinics, and roads.
  • 1978 — Dies in office on 22 August in Mombasa, aged approximately 80. Buried at Parliament Buildings, Nairobi.

His enduring legacy

Kenyatta's legacy is woven into the fabric of modern Kenya. Jomo Kenyatta International Airport in Nairobi bears his name, as does Jomo Kenyatta University of Agriculture and Technology. Kenyatta Day (20 October) is a national public holiday. His portrait appeared on Kenya's banknotes for decades, and his rallying call of “Harambee!” remains Kenya's official national motto.

He transformed a British colony into a confident, sovereign nation and gave Kenyans a language of self-determination that echoed across the entire African continent.
